From 9 Months to 3 Weeks — How AI Factory Rewrites MES Customization

10 Jun 2026 · Youssef A. · 8 min read
cover image · 16:9

We spent three weeks embedded at a tier-1 automotive supplier running Delmia Apriso across two plants. The mandate: cut the average BPM customization cycle from a quarter to a sprint. Here's an honest account of what worked, what didn't, and what the AI co-pilot actually changed on the ground.

The problem with MES customization cycles

Nine months is not an exaggeration. That's the median we saw in our pre-sales discovery across twelve Apriso customers. The time breaks down roughly as: three months scoping the business rule, two months in vendor-DSL scripting, two months in QA, two months in change-management and sign-off. Only the scripting part is intrinsically hard — everything else is mechanical or organizational.

Virtus AI Factory targets the mechanical part. It reads the existing BPM topology, understands the schema, and writes the first draft of the customization with you. It doesn't replace the engineer; it removes the blank-page friction that turns a two-week job into a two-month one.

What the co-pilot actually does

Reads your schema, not a generic one

Most LLM-assisted coding tools have zero context about your MES. AI Factory ingests your Apriso schema, your existing BPM library, and your scripting conventions before it writes a single line. The output looks like your codebase — not a generic example from the internet.

Generates and reviews in the same loop

The engineer describes the rule in plain language. The model proposes a BPM step and its script. The engineer reviews, edits, or rejects. Every rejection trains the session context — by step five the suggestions are narrow enough that review takes thirty seconds, not thirty minutes.

"It felt like pair programming with someone who had read every Apriso script we'd ever written — except it didn't complain about the 6 AM start time." — Lead MES engineer, pilot plant

Where the human is still essential

Two areas remained firmly in human hands throughout the pilot: cross-plant data governance decisions, and anything touching safety-critical steps in the process order. The model flags these correctly — it surfaces them as "requires human review" rather than proposing an answer — which is exactly the right behaviour.

The numbers

  • Average scripting time per BPM step: dropped from 4.2 days to 0.9 days.
  • Defect rate at first QA pass: down 34% vs. the six-month baseline.
  • Engineer ramp time on an unfamiliar module: cut from three weeks to four days.

The QA improvement surprised us most. It turns out that much of the defect budget in a manual customization cycle comes from copy-paste errors in boilerplate script — exactly the thing the model doesn't do.

What's next

We're rolling Opcenter support into the same engine. The schema is different, the DSL is different, the core loop is identical. If your plant runs either platform and you want to run a pilot, talk to us.

Zero Paper Between Driver and Finance — A Fleet Pilot Debrief

28 May 2026 · Rim B. · 6 min read
cover image · 16:9

A last-mile operator running 60 trucks out of Casablanca agreed to a full eight-week pilot of Virtus Fleet. No staged rollout, no parallel spreadsheet — they committed to the dispatch board on day one. Here's the debrief.

Before: the spreadsheet that ran the operation

Before Fleet, dispatch lived in a shared Excel workbook that had grown over six years into something no single person understood completely. It held route assignments, driver contact numbers, vehicle maintenance notes, and a manual fuel log, all in separate tabs linked by fragile formulas.

The finance team received paper delivery receipts from drivers at the end of each shift, manually keyed them into a billing spreadsheet, and invoiced customers weekly. The error rate on invoices was estimated at 11% — they knew because customers called about it regularly.

Week 1–2: migration and training

We imported the vehicle registry, driver list, and customer database in two days. The dispatch team spent the first week running both systems in parallel — Fleet on the left monitor, Excel on the right. By day eight they had stopped opening Excel.

  • Driver app installed on 60 devices in one afternoon (Android, MDM-enrolled).
  • Proof-of-delivery capture live from day three.
  • Fuel log replaced by manual entry in the driver app — took four days to form the habit.

Week 3–8: what actually changed

Dispatch

The dispatch board shows live position, estimated time of arrival, and remaining hours-of-service for every vehicle. Route reassignments that previously required a phone call and a spreadsheet edit now take two clicks. The dispatch manager's assessment after week six: "I used to start every morning wondering where three trucks were. Now I already know."

Finance

POD capture in the driver app feeds directly into the invoicing module. The finance team runs invoice generation on Thursday morning; it takes twelve minutes instead of four hours. Invoice error rate: 1.4% in weeks 5–8, down from 11%.

Invoice error rate dropped from 11% to 1.4% in five weeks. That's not a product claim — it's what the finance manager measured and told us at the week-eight review.

What didn't go as planned

Maintenance scheduling was the roughest part. Three drivers hadn't logged a single maintenance event by week four — they were filing paper forms out of habit and handing them to the workshop manager. We ran a thirty-minute session, and compliance jumped to 85% in week five. The lesson: the app can't fight a paper habit alone; you need one person in the operation who owns the behaviour change.

Results at eight weeks

  • Invoice error rate: 11% → 1.4%
  • Dispatch-to-billing cycle: 5 days → same day
  • Time to onboard a new driver: 3 hours → 45 minutes
  • Spreadsheet opens per week: ~200 → 0

The operator renewed and is expanding to a second depot. Talk to us if you're running a fleet on a spreadsheet stack and want to see the same thing happen.

Three Days in a Freight Dispatch Room

08 Apr 2026 · Rim B. · 5 min read
cover image · 16:9

Field notes are short. They're written the week we come back from a customer site, before the detail fades. This one is from three days in a freight operator's dispatch room — the kind of observation sprint we run before every pilot.

Day 1 — The morning rush

We arrived at 05:45. Dispatch starts at 06:00. The first thing that struck us: three dispatchers, one monitor showing a mapping tool, and one very large printed spreadsheet taped to the wall. The spreadsheet was updated by hand with a marker every morning. It showed which trucks were on which route and which drivers were scheduled.

At 06:17, two drivers called in sick. The dispatcher handling it didn't open any software. She looked at the wall, looked at her phone contacts, and called two backup drivers from memory. The reassignment took eleven minutes. She told us she could do it faster if she didn't have to update the wall sheet afterwards.

Day 2 — The Excel file

We asked to see "the system". There were two: a TMS the company pays a licence for (used primarily to generate the customer-facing invoice PDF), and a shared Excel file hosted on a NAS drive. The Excel file is the real system. It has seventeen tabs. Six of them have the word "OLD" in the name but are still referenced by formulas in the active tabs. Nobody wants to touch it.

"We know it breaks sometimes. We just restart it and it works again. Nobody knows why." — Dispatcher, day two

The fuel log tab is filled in manually every evening by a dispatcher pulling fuel receipts from a physical tray on the desk. The tray had receipts going back three weeks.

Day 3 — The afternoon handover

At 14:30, the day-shift dispatcher handed over to the evening crew. The handover was a ten-minute verbal briefing and a printed sheet of outstanding deliveries. No software state was transferred. If the evening dispatcher needed context on a delivery dispute, she called the day dispatcher on her personal mobile.

We counted seven such calls between 15:00 and 19:00 on day three.

What we took away

  • The dispatchers were excellent operators working around bad tooling, not bad operators who needed to be replaced.
  • The wall spreadsheet existed because the digital tools didn't give a fast enough overview at a glance. Any replacement needs to nail the "wall view" first.
  • The Excel file is the real database. Migration needs to import it faithfully, not dismiss it.
  • The verbal handover is a symptom of missing shift-state persistence — a feature, not a nice-to-have.

This site became a Fleet pilot four weeks later. The wall spreadsheet came down in week two.
